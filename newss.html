Solid Waste and Marine Litter Management

The UNEP Caribbean Environment Programme focuses on tackling marine litter and poor solid waste management, major causes of pollution in the region. Most marine litter, especially plastics, comes from land-based waste that enters waterways due to weak waste collection and recycling systems. The programme works to improve waste management, reduce marine plastics, and support projects such as community cleanups, studies on plastic pollution in fish, and regional partnerships like the “Trash Free Waters” initiative in Jamaica and Panama. It emphasizes collaboration, innovation, and stronger infrastructure to protect Caribbean marine ecosystems and coastal economies.

What will we do with the trash?

The Ocean Cleanup focuses on turning collected plastic waste into valuable resources through proper waste management and valorization. Ocean plastics, which are often degraded, are processed under strict traceability and certification standards, while river waste is handled through local partnerships based on available infrastructure. The organization transforms reusable plastics into durable products, like sunglasses made from ocean plastic, and ensures non-recyclable waste is responsibly disposed of through landfills or waste-to-energy systems. Their main goal is to create value from waste and reduce pollution by viewing waste as a resource, not just debris.

Effective Plastic Waste Solutions

The article by TONTOTON covers the urgent challenge of plastic waste entering oceans, and outlines key strategies to tackle the problem: reducing plastic production and consumption (especially single-use plastics), strengthening waste collection and recycling systems, implementing extended producer responsibility (EPR) schemes, fostering community-led clean-ups and education, advancing innovation in materials and recycling technologies, and promoting international cooperation and strong policy frameworks. It stresses that while these solutions show promise, significant obstacles like high costs, political will and behavioural change remain.
